Solar System

The Sun

Our solar system's central star and energy source, a G-type main-sequence star that contains 99.86% of the system's mass. It's approximately 4.6 billion years old and will continue shining for about another 5 billion years.

Mercury

The closest planet to the Sun and the smallest in our solar system. Its surface is heavily cratered and similar in appearance to our Moon. It has no atmosphere and experiences extreme temperature variations.

Venus

The hottest planet in our solar system, with a thick atmosphere that traps heat in a runaway greenhouse effect. Surface temperatures can reach a scorching 900°F (475°C), hot enough to melt lead.

Earth

Our home planet, the only known world that supports life. It has a protective atmosphere, liquid water, and a perfect distance from the Sun to maintain habitable conditions for millions of species.

Mars

The red planet, known for its rusty iron-rich soil. With polar ice caps, seasons, and evidence of ancient flowing water, Mars is a primary target for future human exploration and potential colonization.

Jupiter

The largest planet in our solar system, a gas giant with a mass 2.5 times that of all other planets combined. Its iconic Great Red Spot is a massive storm that has been raging for hundreds of years.

Saturn

Famous for its spectacular ring system, which consists of ice particles, rocky debris, and dust. Despite its massive size, Saturn has the lowest density of all planets and would float in water if there were an ocean large enough.

Uranus

An ice giant with a unique axial tilt of nearly 98 degrees, causing it to rotate on its side. This gives Uranus extreme seasons, with each pole getting 42 years of continuous sunlight followed by 42 years of darkness.

Neptune

The farthest known planet from the Sun, a cold ice giant with the strongest winds in the solar system, reaching speeds of over 1,200 mph. It was the first planet located through mathematical predictions rather than observation.
